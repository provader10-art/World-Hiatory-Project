About Russia

Russia is the largest country in the world, spanning both Europe and Asia, with a population of about 145 million people, most of whom live in the western, European part near major cities like Moscow and Saint Petersburg, while the eastern regions are desnsily populated, the country faces several major issues, including a declining and aging population due to low birth rates, health challenges such as high rates of disease and lower life expectancy, economic dependence on oil and gas, regional inequality between developed urban areas and less-developed rural regions, environmental problems like pollution and melting permafrost, and ongoing political tensions that affect its economy and global relationships.

More Information

Russia has an estimated population of 140 million (2025) with an urbanized population of 75.3%, with most people living in the western part of the country. The population is aging, with the median age being 42.3 years, and the percentage of the population aged 65+ being 17.8%. The country also has a low fertility rate of 1.52 children born to each woman, with the population growth rate being negative, or -0.49%, meaning the population is declining. The average lifespan in Russia is 72.3 years, with the death rate being higher (13.93 deaths/1,000 people) compared to the birth rate (8.27 births/1,000 people). Russia has very high literacy levels (99.9%), and its citizens have relatively high access to healthcare facilities, although there are problems with obesity (23.1%), tobacco use (26.5%), and moderate levels of alcohol consumption. The country is ethnically diverse but mainly Russian (77.7%), and its predominant language is Russian. The country has diverse religious views, although many people are considered to be non-practicing. The infrastructure has very high levels of clean drinking water (97.1%), and people have access to sanitation facilities (89.4%). The country has moderate levels of expenditure on education and health in relation to its GDP. Russia is facing major demographic challenges despite its high literacy levels and basic infrastructure. The country is facing an aging population and has very low birth and death rates. ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- The economy has several problems happening at the same time, like rising prices, not enough good jobs, big gaps between rich and poor, high government debt, slow growth, and problems getting goods where they need to go. Prices going up makes it harder for people to afford things, while some people struggle to find stable work or earn enough money. At the same time, a small group of people hold most of the wealth, and governments often spend more money than they have. These problems can happen because of things like global events, new technology, and supply issues. To fix them, groups like the Federal Reserve try to control prices, and governments work on creating jobs, improving schools and roads, and making taxes fairer, while organizations like the International Monetary Fund help countries manage money problems. However, fixing the economy is not easy because solving one problem can sometimes make another one harder.
